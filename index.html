    • RULES IN CAPITALIZATION

    8 Capitalization Rules

    1. Capitalize the first word of a sentence

    EXAMPLE: Wake up early

    2. Capitalize the names and other proper nouns

    EXAMPLE: 
Maraya, Andrei P.
Carlos, Mariedel A.
Hernacio, Princess Andrea

    3. Don't capitalize after a colon (usually)

    EXAMPLE: 
I have one true passion: figure-skating. 
2. Maggeie wears a brimmed cap at all times for these two reason: Strong light often gives her a headache. She also like the way it looks.

    4. Capitalize the first word of a quote (sometimes)

    EXAMPLE: 
• Complete sentences  
  Mario asked," What is everyone doing this weekend?"
• Partial sentences
  Andrea said she was "way too busy " to join the gym

    5. Capitalize day, months, and holidays, but not season

    Days, months, and holidays are always capitalized as these are proper nouns.

    6. Capitalize most word in title

    EXAMPLE: 
• First word
• Nouns
• Verbs 
• Adjective 
• Proper nouns

    7. Capitalize cities, countries, nationalities, and language

    8. Capitalize time period and events (sometimes)

    EXAMPLE: 
The Roaring Twenties or the American Civil War.

    C. SPELLING RULES

    “ie” and “ei

    • “ie” is general truth
• "i” before “e” except after “c”
EXAMPLE:
  believe
  field
  achieve

    • Write “e” before “i” when it is after “c”
EXAMPLE:
  receive
  ceiling
  deceive

    • Spell “-ei” when the combination has the sound of a long ā, as in weigh (wā).
EXAMPLE:
  neighbor
  veil
  vein

    • Some words that spelled “ie” instead of “ei”
EXAMPLE:
  caffeine
  protein
  either

    • Some words that spelled “ie” even after “c”
EXAMPLE:
  receive vs. efficient
  deceive vs. ancient
  ceiling vs. proficient

    Words with double letters

    • Double-letter words with 3 letters
EXAMPLE:
  add, see, off, zoo, ill, egg

    • Double-letter words with 4 letters
EXAMPLE:
  pool, cook, book, cool, boom

    • Double-letter words with 5 letters
EXAMPLE:
  bunny, class, balls, cooks, chill

    • Double-letter words with 6 letters
EXAMPLE:
  appear, affect, bamboo, appeal, common

    • Longer double-letter words because of suffixes and prefixes
EXAMPLE:
  accused, appealing, commentator, communication, procession

    • Words that start with double letters
EXAMPLE:
  eerie, eel, ooze

    • Words that end with double letters
EXAMPLE:
  class, cell, chess, dress, fill

    • Words with 2 sets of double letters
EXAMPLE:
  coffee, bittersweet, mattress, balloon, embarrass

    • Words with 3 sets of double letters
EXAMPLE:
  bookkeeping, possessiveness, addressee, greenness, aggressiveness

    • Words with 3 sets of double letters
EXAMPLE:
  bookkeeping, possessiveness, addressee, greenness, aggressiveness

    NOTE:

    • There is no size requirement for a word
    • The larger of word, the more you see your vocabulary development
    • It is necessary to emphasize the certain words

    Rules in double-letter words with suffixes and prefixes

    • Double final consonant (-ed and -ing)
EXAMPLE:
  stop = stopped
  clog = clogged
  run = running

    • 1.1.1 Rule (1 syllable, 1 vowel, and 1 consonant)
EXAMPLE:
  run = running
1 syllable, 1 vowel, 1 consonant
  dream = dreamming or dreamed
1 syllable, 2 vowels, 1 consonant

    • Doubling letters in adding prefixes are not common but they do exist
EXAMPLE:
 necessary = unnecessary
 operate = cooperate

BUT WHY?
occur = occurring or occurred when it does not follow the 1.1.1 rule
forget = forgetting
refer = referring
control = controlled
refer = referred

    NOTE:

    • It is fine to double the last consonant of a word even if it has two or more syllables IF AND ONLY IF THE LAST SYLLABLE IS STRESSED.
    • But be aware that even if a word follows the 1.1.1 rule and it has a stressed syllable, there are still some consonants that do not need to be doubled.

    Consonants that require to double: b, d, f, g, l, m, n, r, p, and t Consonants that do not require to double: x, w, y, k, and h

    PLURALIZING NOUNS ENDING WITH O, F, AND Y

    Let's recap!

Noun- Is a name of a person, animal, place, things, or an event.
Singular noun- referring to single name (dog, cat, or rabbit)
Plural noun- referring to two or more names (dogs, cats, or rabbits)
Prefix- is an additional part of a word that comes at the beginning the words to identify the meaning
Suffix- Is an additional part of a word that comes at the end of the words to identify the meaning

    Pluralizing nouns ending with o

    • Add “-s” if the end of the word ending with vowels
EXAMPLE:
  radios, photos, stereos, tattoos

    • Add “-s” if the word ends with consonant and o
EXAMPLE:
  photos, pianos, zeros

    • Add “-s” if the word is a foreign language
EXAMPLE:
  kimonos, tacos

    • Add “-s” if the word is a musical or instrumental terms 
EXAMPLE:
 pianos, sopranos, altos

    • Add “-s” if the words is a short term/abbreviation 
EXAMPLE:
 photos, kilos, autos, memos

    • But some words are pluralized with “es”. It can be because “o” is followed after the consonant
• But both “s” and “es” in a word are acceptable
EXAMPLE:
  Tomatoes, mangoes, piano, potatoes

    Pluralizing words ending with F/FE

    • Change “f/fe” into “v/-ves” at the end of the word with f or fe
EXAMPLE:
  life = lives
  leaf = leaves
  knife = knives
  wife = wives

    Pluralizing words ending with y

    • Add “-ies” if the word is preceded by consonant
EXAMPLE:
  baby = babies
  hobby = hobbies
  city = cities

    • But add “-s” if the word is preceded by vowels before “y”, add “s” and it matters.
EXAMPLE:
  boy = boys
  toy = toys
  ray = rays

    BRITISH ENGLISH VS. AMERICAN ENGLISH

    BRITISH ENGLISH
    • British English spelling have spark from the other languages such as German and French
    • English was brought by Americans in 17th Century
    • Samuel Johnson published “A Dictionary of the English Language” with 40,000 words in 1755 lasted for 8 years.
    AMERICAN ENGLISH
    • American English spelling were based on the actual sounds when spoken
    • Noah Webster published “An American Dictionary of the English Language” in 1828 with 70,000 words.

    See the following words of British and American English, their differences and uniqueness.

    BRITISH ENGLISH AMERICAN ENGLISH 
    In spelling of end -or, British spelling is “our” and English spelling “-or”
EXAMPLE:
colour = colour 
flavour = flavor
neighbour = neighbor
    		 
    In spelling -ize, British spelling is either “-ize” or “-ise” and English is “-ize”

    ITALIC RULES

    Use italics when composing on a computer. Underline when writing by hand.

1. Italicize all titles of long works (books, magazines, journals, newspapers, plays, long poems)
EXAMPLE:
Dante's Inferno is one of my favorite long poems.

Note: DO NOT italicize the titles of major religious texts, books of the Bible, or classic legal documents.

2. Italicize the names of ships, trains, aircraft, and spacecraft. 
EXAMPLE:
I plan a trip on the Norwegian Spirit next year .

Note: DO NOT italicize types of ships, trains, aircraft.
EXAMPLE:
Boeing 767; Trident submarine

3. Italicize the names of films, TV shows, albums, paintings, sculptures.
EXAMPLE:
Avatar continues to break records.
My favorite Beatles' album is The White Album.

4. Italicize the Latin names of genus and species
EXAMPLE:
Homo sapiens

5. Italicize foreign words and phrases that have not become accepted words of English vocabulary
EXAMPLE:
The classic longest word in German, Donaudampfschifffahrtsgesellschaftskapitän, has 42 letters. "Danube steamship company captain."

Note: Words such as patio, gumbo, spaghetti, and résumé that have become accepted English words are not italicized.

Common abbreviated Latin terms are also not italicized
Ex: etc., et al., e.g., i.e.

6. Italicize a letter, word, number, or expression to emphasize or clarify.
EXAMPLE:
Does that word begin with an f or a ph?
The word weird is often misspelled
"That might be what you believe!" she replied.
